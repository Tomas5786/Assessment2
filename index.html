An interview with an IT professional

As a new student in any field can attest, learning something completely new is full of the unknown. I know this only too well. Not only now five weeks ago I began my journey in IT, a subject I only recently realised despite my preconceptions, I know little to nothing about. Like with any discipline, what better way to learn than to hear from those who have gone through what you have. Those who are now the leaders in their field and have battled the same battles you are fighting now. Today I interview Dr Matt Johnson, a Senior AI engineer at Showroom in Tokyo, Japan.


What kind of work do you do as an IT professional?

I've been doing this now. I don't know, 15 years or so. I've worked on quite a few projects up until now. I did a master's in computer graphics and then I did my PhD in like 2010 in big data and data analytics and AI. Machine learning became really popular on then. After that, I've worked on just a variety of different AI projects and AI related projects. AI became big. And then there's a bunch of articles that come out that tell a bunch of mid-level managers that if their organization isn't using AI for this, or that, or big data for this, or that, you know, they're going to go bankrupt any day now. So, yeah, I mean, most of my work has kind of been down that vein of development. Most of the stuff that I've done is kind of machine learning oriented. So, you get a bunch of data. And then Alright, like, how can we extract some statistical knowledge out of this, right, that's going to be useful that we can apply. But there's an enormous number of sub fields that are available, right? Prior to that I did robotics, it's drone flight, autonomous drone flight, there's two big problems that I worked on in that area. One is just navigation. I mean, how do you do path planning in 3d space? In games, they do it a lot. In 2d space, essentially. Right? They ignore, things that you would bump your head into. What is cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting hardware, sensitive data, software, and critical systems from digital attacks. Information technology security, also known as IT security places defence measures, to prevent networked systems and applications from being attacked, whether it is from inside or outside of an organisation (Shea, Gillis and Clark, 2021)

Professional organisations are just as guilty of this too, from sending emails to work colleagues and clients with sensitive information without appropriate protection in place or posting confidential data through messages on unprotected platforms. MSPs are also started utilising Artificial intelligence (AI) more into their security systems in recent years to help combat cyber strikes made towards corporations. AI security tools are used for detection, predication, and response to cybercriminal threats. However, for the time being a lot of organisations are not migrating towards this approach, and therefore are still falling victims to ransomware attacks. This is because not all business can afford the hefty price tag that comes along with such a service. Fully autonomous cars, while an exciting prospect for not only drivers are not without issues, particularly moral ones. The trolley problem is a famous ethical dilemma in which a person has to decide whether to save five people by switching a lever and kill one or to not act and kill five. To summarise, should a child trip while crossing the road, should the autonomous vehicle kill the child or the "driver". If it kills the child, then who is culpable. If we kill the "driver", considering the driver is not the cause of the child tripping over, is it right to kill the "driver" who for all intents and purposes is simply a passenger? At the current time, robotics has been able to produce five types of robots (Builtin.com. 2022). These include:


  1. Pre Programmed Robots: A pre-programmed robot operates and works on simple tasks, in controlled environments.

  2. Humanoid Robots:Humanoid robots are robots made to look like, act like, and perform tasks like humans. They can run, walk, jump, and carry things. Some of them also have human-like faces and expressions.

  3. Autonomous Robots:An autonomous robot can operate and work on its own, without human interaction. They usually carry out tasks that do not involve any human supervision and do so due to having sensors installed that help with perception of the world around them.

  4. Teleoperated Robots:Teleoperated robots are controlled from a safe distance by a human, done so by a wireless network. These robots are used to explore, operate and work in environments that are otherwise too harsh for humans.

  5. Augmenting Robots:Augmenting robots are robots who can enhance human capabilities or completely replace a capability a human has lost. The Robotics field is hoping to enhance these further as these may be the future of robots. Enhancing human life quality by replacing otherwise irreplaceable things or making humans stronger, faster & tougher.

All these robots can be used, distributed, and worked with at present. Robots are currently used in a variety of different fields and industries. These include healthcare, medicine, science, space exploration, surgery, packaging, manufacturing, and construction. They can help you make music, make food, improve your vision, read your mood, serve as companions, help you mow the lawn - The list is endless. However, the vast majority of robots are used today to help humans with manufacturing and labor tasks. They are used more so for industrial tasks that are otherwise too extensive and expensive for humans to perform, or in some cases, downright impossible, such as space exploration.


In the near future, robots will be used alongside A.I (Artificial Intelligence) to make our lives a lot easier, both inside the home and outside. The Robotics community is working on having self-driving and autonomous transport readily available for both personal use and public use (Ex. self-driving taxis). Alongside the IoT community, the robotics community is also working with A.I to create robots who will be able to connect to other robots - something known as a robotic ecosystem. These robots will have robust A.I capability, full connectivity and will be able to use other robotic machines, connect to the cloud and analyze data.


Robotics today has progressed exponentially - We are no longer at the “assembly line” due to A.I impacting every sector of the robotics field. Robots are increasingly becoming more autonomous and aware of the world around them and how to interact with humans. This is due to the several (but not limited to) A.I technologies.


These A.I Technologies include:


  • Computer Vision: Technology that allows robots to understand and “see” the visual world using deep learning models and digital images.
  • Natural Language Processing: Technology that allows robots to understand human language.
  • Edge Computing: Alongside the A.I at the Cloud, Edge is used for the processing of data near the source (point of origin) rather than transmitting data to the cloud.
  • Complex Event Processing: Combines data from various sources, with the goal of responding to an event as quickly as possible.
  • Transfer Learning and A.I: Transfer learning refers to using knowledge gained from solving one problem and reapplying that knowledge to fix a similar or related problem.
  • Force sensing
  • Speech recognition

Project Idea - E-Ink Car Display

Overview

To help display information about the car quickly and easily to other drivers via an E-Ink display mounted on the rear and front of the vehicle. We would offer two removable screens. These would be placed on the windscreen or onto other easy to view locations on the front and back of vehicle. These displays would be embedded or mounted on the bumpers and can be used collaboratively or on their own. An example of the product being utilised, would be a family with 2 children, the youngest now has their Learners licence and needs to display their L plates. The eldest child has their Green or Red P plates and cannot drive the vehicle without changing plates. This would allow the family to change between the two different plates with ease by using an integrated app built into the cars Operating System or via an easy-to-use Android/iOS app that can be downloaded via the OS’s respective store and synchronised with the displays via Bluetooth. 


It will also allow for the display of novelty plates like Baby on Board without the need to hang one of those signs on the front and back windscreens. Also implementing a feature such as an app for customising and controlling what Is displayed on the monitors would also allow for firmware updates and the introduction of new features in the future. Being able to provide our customers with firmware updates would not only give them peace of mind that they are getting the newest and best product care, but would also allow the implantation of required changes made to meet lawful requirements as traffic rules change.


The mobile app would also be able to have more customisable options such as colour, font, and personalised badges. Having the option to add personalised badges would allow other drives on the road know what groups you support such as, a charity foundation or sports teach. Customizations on the app also allow the user to set a day and night cycle to the display screens, this would allow the change in brightness, colour, or even font to help other road users see better at night or in visual impairing circumstances.



Motivation

The initial idea behind “baby on board” signs were to notify search and rescue parties of the presence of a young child in the advent of an accident. According to the Australian Road Death Database (2018), amongst children 0-14 years old, 34 deaths were due to road traffic accidents, a rate of 0.7 per 100,000 children. The most common causes of injury death for young children and babies are land transport accidents, followed closely by accidental drowning and assault. Car accidents contribute a significant amount to the death toll in Australia. If a child was to be thrown from a vehicle in an accident and such a sign was not displayed, there is no guarantee the child may be found in time to save his/her life. “Baby on Board” signs do not have a monopoly on in-car window displays. A learner driver must still display their “L” plates, a provisional driver, their “P’s.” .



If you were to display such a sign in your car, what is the salient reason for doing so? They “communicate” to other drivers the current situation of your car. If “L” or “P” plates are displayed, it communicates to other drivers to give you a wide berth. In 2007, new laws were introduced that affected P-Platers. Laws stated that provisional drivers were not allowed to use their mobile phones in the car, as well as carry passengers between the hours of 11pm and 5am. Of more than 1300 P-Platers booked soon after the introduction of this law, 1280 drivers were not properly displaying their plates. With the addition of the E-Ink Display, P-Platers, and L-Platers will be forced to display, i.e., by default, their plates due to having it connected to their certain car profiles via Apple Play and Android Auto. In this way, parents can monitor their young adults and teenagers when they are driving by setting up these profiles, so when they enter the car, the respective plate will be displayed. Additionally, “P” and “L” platers having their plates displayed will reduce the instances of unlawful behaviour whilst driving, including driving with an illegal blood alcohol count of 0.01 (one standard drink) and the use of mobile phone. That is, talking on the phone, having the phone held in hand or messaging. In turn, this will reduce the number of road car accidents involving P or L platers as they are 30 times more likely to crash than an individual on their full license.


Why stop here? What else can be communicated to other drivers? In Australia, dangerous goods are allocated to 9 different classes. Explosives, gases, flammable liquids, flammable solids; substances liable to spontaneous combustion; substances which, on contact with water, emit flammable gases, oxidizing substances and organic peroxides, toxic and infectious substances, radioactive material, corrosive substances, miscellaneous dangerous substances, and articles. The E-Ink Display can be changed on the go depending on what a truck driver is carrying – increasing efficiency when doing long travel runs. Additionally, this information will be clearly communicated with other drivers reducing the chances of road rage incidents when drivers encounter a slow-moving vehicle. Around 72% of Australians have had another driver yell or scream at them whilst driving. More than three quarters of students (More likely to be P-Platers) said that they would get aggressive with slow drivers if they experience some sort of road rage. Displaying a clear “UN No.1203 (Petrol – Flammable Liquid)” sign for example, allows for peace of mind as the situation is clearly communicated to other drivers.



The E-Ink Car Display is an extension of this philosophy.


In conjunction with this philosophy, we already communicate so much information using indicators. You want to turn left, you move your indicator up and alert other drivers that you intend to make a left turn, conversely, you want to turn right, you move the indicator shaft down. If you are the driver at the tail of a traffic jam, you use your hazard lights.


It was also to help minimise the use of stick-on signs used to display council parking permits, disabled parking permits or any other information that needs to be displayed. It could also be used to display the parking ticket in public parking areas that require a user to pay for parking after a certain time limit.



Description

A compact and removable E-Ink display attached to the rear and front windscreens or embedded on the number plates attachment area of the vehicle that displays information to other motorists on the road via image or text. The information displayed on the screens can be immediately, and simply configured through a mobile application. The app will be available to both Apple and Android users, with connectivity to Apple Play and Android Auto. The E-Ink displays will be using hardware such as Arduino to connect via Bluetooth to output your choice of display image or massage from your smartphone. Your smartphone can be operated hands free via Siri or Google Assistant while driving to control the app and update what is on the display while you are driving.

Software Development Tools - App Development

The app will be built as a using Visual Studio tools for Xamarin and written in C#. This is the most efficient and well covered development option that lets native apps be developed with .Net. The app will consist of the one code base written in C# for multiple platforms and this allows testing and deployment for all apps to be done from the one location.

Software Development Tools - Hardware Development

The E-Ink Display will be controlled by an Arduino device as the central controlling device of the hardware that has an additional Bluetooth and LCD Display modules. Arduino hardware and software is open source which allows us to use the hardware as a foundation and modifications to the hardware can be made without additional licencing or third-party costs.


Hardware design and building will be done in KiCad which is a free CAD (Computer Aided Design) software tool that will develop the E-Ink Displays hardware schematics, PCBs and layouts for manufacturing all in the one software. Using this tool will enable the hardware development from design, schematic testing, PCB design and then manufacturing as it can link to different PCB manufacturers enabling us to source the most cost effective and fast manufacturers. It also enables us to change manufactures should it be required.


Visual Studio code will be used to develop the hardware code for the Arduino Device. This uses a C++ like language that has open libraries readily available for Bluetooth connectivity and LCD Display hardware components. Developing in Visual Studio code will integrate with the Arduino IDE, DevOps and with GitHub allowing the code to be used by the hardware to be tasked and tracked through DevOps Task Boards as well as being backed up to the cloud and version controlled through GitHub.


Hardware Required

  • 2x Colour E-Ink Display measuring around 145mm by 145mm minimum. (7inch TFT/ LCD Display NX8048K070 – Nextion 7.0” Enhanced Series HMI Display)
  • 2 x Screen mounts for Front and Back of car (Removable)
  • 2 x Number plate mount (embedded)
  • Arduino Nano (nano-v3-0-atmega328p)
  • Bluetooth Module (HC-06) – Peripheral only
  • Mini USB to car power supply cable.
  • Plastic Enclosure to protect the screen, hardware and allow for easy attachment or removal of mounts

Hardware Manufacturing

After the initial prototypes, manufacturing of the hardware will consist of 4 stages:


  • PCB Manufacturing: PCB will come fully assembled with all components surface mounted using SMT. It will be manufactured through www.jlcpcb.com where our schematics made in KiCad can be uploaded with the selected components from this manufacturer. This is a leading global PCB manufacture that can produce high quality PCBs fully assembled with all components for a low cost.
  • Enclosure manufacturing: The PCB and components such as the screen will need to be enclosed in a case for protection, safety and to prevent tapering. EInk will use a JLCPCB to design and produce 3d printed enclosers based on our PCB and hardware requirements. After the initial release if demand has increased, injection moulded plastic versions of the enclosures will be created.
    Injection moulding has a high initial cost ($3000 USD+) for the design and creation of a mould (typically made of machined steel), so this isn’t feasible immediately but will considered and a covered cost if demand requires more than a 500-1000 units P/A to be produced.
  • Hardware Assembly: PCB’s, LCD Displays and Enclosures will be assembled at a in house manufacturing facility initially where the software will also be applied to the hardware. Once demand increases the hardware assembly can be outsourced to the PCB and Enclosure manufacturer as this is only an option with a large supply request.
  • Software updating: After receiving the PCB’s and enclosures from the manufacturer, the software will be imaged to the hardware via USB per device in house initially. After demand increases, so will the manipulating scale and this software imaging will be done during the PCB manufacturing stage. This comes at a higher cost that won’t be considered until a high demand is met that can cover these costs.
