Autonomous Vehicles

Abstract :

Autonomous vehicles are the way of the future. We as motorists already have witnessed incremental advancements in vehicle safety features such as automatic emergency braking and such other technologies. With the territory comes moral and ethical dilemmas which must be dealt with as we keep pushing the boundaries between human and autonomous drivers which begs the question as what legislators will do about licencing and who can be chaperoned in a self-driving car. There is always the question of which occupations will be lost and conversely, will jobs be created in the void created by the jobs lost.

The current state of autonomous vehicles.

The evolution of technology is analogous with the evolution of life. Incremental changes over time with each goal or success merely a steppingstone to the next stage of the journey. Road travelling vehicles are no different, from the horse drawn cart to the Model T Ford, from the first electric car to self-driving vehicles. Once thought of as science fiction and featured in a number of past films that dreamed about the future such as The Minority Report from 2002 and Demolition Man from 1993, self-driving autonomous vehicles are undoubtedly the way of the future. Many cars currently have some form of automation already included as standard; automatic emergency braking system and lane centring assist such as first introduced by the innovative Subaru Eye-Sight® and self-parking vehicles such as the Tesla Model 3 are two such examples. Currently, according to The Society of Automotive Engineers (2018) from totally human controlled vehicles to fully autonomous vehicles, there are six levels of autonomy in motor vehicles. Fully autonomous affordable consumer vehicles that will get the "passenger" from home to work, freeing the passenger to catch up on meetings or relax watching the morning news or catch up on their favourite shows on a streaming service might be several years off yet, however with the incremental improvements leading towards this, it will surely be less a dream of sci-fi writers, more of a reality within many of our lifetimes.

What are the moral and ethical implications of self-driving vehicles?

Fully autonomous cars, while an exciting prospect for not only drivers are not without issues, particularly moral ones. An argument can be made that taking the control of the car out of the "drivers" hands would solve many issues such as culpability in case of accidents. Also, it may allow those who are too far advanced in age to "drive" on much longer than is currently possible. According to Road Safety Transport NSW, drivers must complete a medical check-up two months before and every year after turning 75. Post 85, a yearly driving test is also required. One must wonder if an elderly person who normally would be in a compromised medical state was to buy a fully autonomous car, should they be allowed to "drive" it.  Conversely, if a car is self-driving, could a child be allowed to hop in the family car and be driven to the shopping centre?

The trolley problem is a famous ethical dilemma in which a person has to decide whether to save five people by switching a lever and kill one or to not act and kill five. It's an ethical dilemma because while inaction on the surface might seem as such, it is still a conscious decision. When it comes to autonomous vehicles, this dilemma is one that must be overcome. The Tunnel Problem, a term coined by Millar(n.d), is similar to the trolley problem. To summarise, should a child trip while crossing the road, should the autonomous vehicle kill the child or the "driver". If it kills the child, then who is culpable. Coming to a moral position on this leads to somewhat of a circular rabbit hole for lack of a better term. If we murder the child, can we claim self-defence? If we kill the "driver", considering the driver is not the cause of the child tripping over, is it right to kill the "driver" who for all intents and purposes is simply a passenger? Similarly, if the car is forced to choose between killing a child or an elderly grandmother, morally can we put more value on the life of a child? This will be up to legislators to decide on. However, this may be a moot point if cars turn out to be a much safer option. Shwartz (2021) writes; "More than 35,000 people die every year in motor vehicle crashes in the US alone (Updated: NHTSA reports 42,915 motorvehicle deaths in 2021, and increase of 10.5% from the previous year). Since self-driving vehicles can theoretically react faster than human drivers and do not drive drunk, text while driving, or get tired, they should be able to dramatically improve vehicle safety. They also promise to increase the independence and mobility of seniors and others who cannot easily drive." Considering all the accidents listed above are caused by human error. The tunnel problem may be an unavoidable one, but will lives be saved considering that accidents traditionally caused by human error may be heavily reduced?

The immediate future of self-driving vehicles.

Considering that countries such as the UK, in 2030 (via highways.today Davis, A (2022)) and Australia, in 2035 (via carsales.com.au) looking to ban petrol fuel vehicles, the race is on to prepare the roads, technology and by no means least the people for an automated vehicular future. So what steps are being made to bring this into fruition? Feuer (2021) claims that computer hardware and software giant Apple is looking to launch a fully self-driving vehicle by 2025, furthermore, one without a steering wheel. American automobile company General Motors are also in the running to launch a fully self-automated electric vehicle in direct competition with Tesla’s autopilot® initiative (Wilmot, S. (2022))

Many human occupations have been made to make way to technology advancements. Traffic controllers to traffic lights, supermarket cashiers to self-checkouts, factory jobs, delivery jobs. The list goes on. One must also consider that although these jobs were lost to technology, jobs were created to, for example, engineers maintain and build the aforementioned traffic lights, self-checkouts, factory robots and delivery drones. When it comes to autonomous vehicles, self-driving trucks are one of the ways jobs will lose out to technological advancements. Mining company Mineral Resources (MR) (2021) are in conjunction with autonomous engineering specialist Hexagon are working on a road train (Australian term for long combination vehicle), to transport ore from far reaching, isolated areas. MR states that driverless road trains will enhance efficiency and reduce risks to drivers due to fatigue and increase fleet availability.

The impact of self-driving vehicles on myself and loved ones.

In the immediate future, such vehicles are unlikely to be affordable. Nevertheless, like most technology such as high-definition TVs, as demand increases, prices generally decline. I can envisage a future where such vehicles are the norm but will staunch drivers refuse to give up on their driving “rights”? Will human driven cars become outlawed once we have fully transitioned to autonomous vehicular travel? Perhaps gone will be the days where parents nervously teach their children to drive, as such tasks will have become obsolete. This may also heavily reduce the instance of traffic accidents leading to death or serious injury making the roads safer for all drivers. In such a scenario will the costs of goods and services be reflected in the cost of living in general?

How autonomous vehicles will affect myself, family and friends is a difficult question to answer. A case can be made that it will make commuting and travelling in cars in general safer. The peace of mind that when my daughter first “drives” alone, it won’t be an endeavour as fraught with danger as it was when I first started driving. Should self-driving road trains be the norm, perhaps goods and services may become less expensive, possible raising living standards allowing for a little more leeway in the household budget.

Conversely, perhaps my family won’t have the budget for a fully autonomous vehicle rendering my family a class below those who can. Should we not be able to afford one, will our respective commute times blow out losing precious time and freedom? Each answer begs a new question, but it is one I am personally very excited to see come to fruition.

Robots

Introduction:


Robots are products of the robotic field. A robot itself is a type of automated machine that is built to perform and mimic tasks done by humans at high precision and speed. Originally, robots were built to perform simple tasks. In 1954, the first programmable and digitally operated robot was invented by George Devol called the Unimate. Sold in 1960 to General Motors, it was one of the first robots to move pieces of hot metal. (History of Robots - Adelaide Robotics Academy, 2022). Since then, robots have drastically evolved beyond their initial uses and have aided in incredible ways – saving lives, cleaning homes, assisting in difficult surgeries, bettering lives through science and engineering. Today, robots can fully autonomously perform tasks on their own, built alongside A.I (Artificial Intelligence) technology which makes for incredibly powerful machines, or be human controlled whereby they complete tasks with human influence.


The current state of robots:


At the current time, robotics has been able to produce five types of robots (Builtin.com. 2022). These include:


  1. Pre Programmed Robots: A pre-programmed robot operates and works on simple tasks, in controlled environments.

  2. Humanoid Robots:Humanoid robots are robots made to look like, act like, and perform tasks like humans. They can run, walk, jump, and carry things. Some of them also have human-like faces and expressions.

  3. Autonomous Robots:An autonomous robot can operate and work on its own, without human interaction. They usually carry out tasks that do not involve any human supervision and do so due to having sensors installed that help with perception of the world around them.

  4. Teleoperated Robots:Teleoperated robots are controlled from a safe distance by a human, done so by a wireless network. These robots are used to explore, operate and work in environments that are otherwise too harsh for humans.

  5. Augmenting Robots:Augmenting robots are robots who can enhance human capabilities or completely replace a capability a human has lost. The Robotics field is hoping to enhance these further as these may be the future of robots. Enhancing human life quality by replacing otherwise irreplaceable things or making humans stronger, faster & tougher.

All these robots can be used, distributed, and worked with at present. Robots are currently used in a variety of different fields and industries. These include healthcare, medicine, science, space exploration, surgery, packaging, manufacturing, and construction. They can help you make music, make food, improve your vision, read your mood, serve as companions, help you mow the lawn - The list is endless. However, the vast majority of robots are used today to help humans with manufacturing and labor tasks. They are used more so for industrial tasks that are otherwise too extensive and expensive for humans to perform, or in some cases, downright impossible, such as space exploration.


In the near future, robots will be used alongside A.I (Artificial Intelligence) to make our lives a lot easier, both inside the home and outside. The Robotics community is working on having self-driving and autonomous transport readily available for both personal use and public use (Ex. self-driving taxis). Alongside the IoT community, the robotics community is also working with A.I to create robots who will be able to connect to other robots - something known as a robotic ecosystem. These robots will have robust A.I capability, full connectivity and will be able to use other robotic machines, connect to the cloud and analyze data.


Robotics today has progressed exponentially - We are no longer at the “assembly line” due to A.I impacting every sector of the robotics field. Robots are increasingly becoming more autonomous and aware of the world around them and how to interact with humans. This is due to the several (but not limited to) A.I technologies.


These A.I Technologies include:


  • Computer Vision: Technology that allows robots to understand and “see” the visual world using deep learning models and digital images.
  • Natural Language Processing: Technology that allows robots to understand human language.
  • Edge Computing: Alongside the A.I at the Cloud, Edge is used for the processing of data near the source (point of origin) rather than transmitting data to the cloud.
  • Complex Event Processing: Combines data from various sources, with the goal of responding to an event as quickly as possible.
  • Transfer Learning and A.I: Transfer learning refers to using knowledge gained from solving one problem and reapplying that knowledge to fix a similar or related problem.
  • Force sensing
  • Speech recognition

The Impact of Robots


The standard of living will change with the addition of robots to the workplace. Professionals will be able to focus on more important tasks and work on upskilling themselves whilst robots perform and work on minor tasks. A wide range of industries will also change, especially the healthcare & education industry.


An article by Small Business Trends states that “the world population over the age of 60 has tripled in the last 50 years according to the United Nations” (5 Ways Robots Are Changing the World - Including Your Business, 2022). Autonomous bots will be used to aid older people in their own homes, ridding them of making the heartbreaking decision of transferring to an aged care facility. This will only provide support and reduce the amount of stress on our aged care facilities and healthcare systems overall. Furthermore, robots are also saving lives with extremely accurate, precise, and small incisions done in surgery that may otherwise be too risky for a human to perform. To date, robots have been used in “20,000 surgeries”. They will also be able to scan humans for any medical issues, be precise in detecting deadly conditions that are otherwise unseen to the naked eye and provide medical advice.


Moving onto the education sector, A.I Tech will also be able to educate future and current generations in a way human teachers cannot. Computer-based learning allows students to experience a more direct, personalized way of teaching at their own pace promoting a healthy education system. Providing support alongside human teachers who cannot physically tutor students on a personalized level, one on one. (5 Ways Robots Are Changing the World - Including Your Business, 2022)


In the hospitality industry, robots are used currently in venues to deliver food and drinks to a table without a need for a waiter. Businesses are slowly adding robots to their organizations due to reliability, and as humans we will need to learn how to work with them side by side.


Overall, with the addition of robots to the workplace, employees can expect to have their jobs transformed. Meaning, although many current jobs will be lost, newer ones will emerge. With the creation of autonomous vehicles for example, companies will instead create new jobs by having drivers become software engineers, rider support operators or driving fleet technicians. It is expected that technology will create “12 million more jobs” than it destroys.


However, economically, it is in discussion what the outcome of robots means for us. Will our wages suffer? Are the predictions of robots creating and transforming MORE jobs than it destroys accurate? It is still too early in development to be certain.

Indeed, working alongside robots will make the lives of some people easier, but it is fair to say that those who lose their jobs to those robots will experience a more difficult life. In different sectors, however, like healthcare - the positives heavily outweigh the negatives. Saving lives that may not be saved by humans is undeniably a great argument to have a robot in the workplace. With the rising life expectancy, it is a fantastic addition to have robots look after our elderly - allowing our healthcare workers to achieve and provide a better service by having less people to look after.


Project Idea - E-Ink Car Display

Overview

To help display information about the car quickly and easily to other drivers via an E-Ink display mounted on the rear and front of the vehicle. We would offer two removable screens. These would be placed on the windscreen or onto other easy to view locations on the front and back of vehicle. These displays would be embedded or mounted on the bumpers and can be used collaboratively or on their own. An example of the product being utilised, would be a family with 2 children, the youngest now has their Learners licence and needs to display their L plates. The eldest child has their Green or Red P plates and cannot drive the vehicle without changing plates. This would allow the family to change between the two different plates with ease by using an integrated app built into the cars Operating System or via an easy-to-use Android/iOS app that can be downloaded via the OS’s respective store and synchronised with the displays via Bluetooth. It will also allow for the display of novelty plates like Baby on Board without the need to hang one of those signs on the front and back windscreens.

Motivation

The initial idea behind the “baby on board” signs were to notify search and rescue parties of the presence of a young child in the advent of an accident. If a child was to be thrown from a vehicle in an accident and such a sign was not displayed, there is no guarantee the child might be found in time to save his/her life. "Baby on Board” signs do not have a monopoly on in-car window displays. A learner driver must display their “L” plates, a provisionary driver, their “P” s.


If you were to display such a sign in your car, what is the salient reason for doing so? They “communicate” to other drivers the current situation of your car. If “L” or “P” plates are displayed, it communicates to other drivers to give you a wide berth. As stated in the previous paragraph, if “Baby on Board” signs are displayed, they alert rescue teams of the possible presence of a child.


Why stop here? What else can be “communicated” to other drivers? Imagine you are driving a truck that has fragile or explosive cargo and need to drive slower than usual. Without communicating this fact to other drivers, it may cause unnecessary frustration, some which may lead to road rage incidents. Display a “UN No. 1203 (Petrol – Flammable Liquid)” on the E-Ink Display and drive with the peace of mind that the situation is being clearly communicated to other drivers.


The E-Ink Car Display is an extension of this philosophy.


In conjunction with this philosophy, we already communicate so much information using indicators. You want to turn left, you move your indicator up and alert other drivers that you intend to make a left turn, conversely, you want to turn right, you move the indicator shaft down. If you are the driver at the tail of a traffic jam, you use your hazard lights.


It was also to help minimise the use of stick-on signs used to display council parking permits, disabled parking permits or any other information that needs to be displayed. It could also be used to display the parking ticket in public parking areas that require a user to pay for parking after a certain time limit.

Description

A small and removable E-Ink display attached to the rear and front windscreens or embedded on the number plates attachment area of the vehicle that displays information to others via image or text.

Tools\Technologies

  • 2 x Colour E-Ink Displays with a minimum measurement of 145mm by 145mm
  • 2 x Screen mounts (removable)
  • 2 x Number plate mount (embedded)
  • Raspberry Pi 4. Minimum 1GB Ram, running Raspberry PiOS or other Linux distribution
  • 256 GB Micro SD Card
    • 32 GB - OS Partition
    • 220 GB - Storage Partition
    • 4 GB - Boot and other partitions
  • Python with CircuitPython Support
  • USB-C to Car Power Supply cable
  • Android Studio with Flutter plugin
  • Web Server, Apache or equivalant
  • NAS or hosted storage

Skills

We would need skills in Python, Linux (Raspberry Pi OS or Ubuntu), general car electronics and the ability to create an Android/iOS application using Android Studio with the Flutter plugin. We will also need the skills to setup a cloud storage site if we are to host it locally, by using a NAS, or be able to integrate the app with Sharepoint.

Outcome

Displaying of different images or text on screens located in the windscreens (removable versions) or the number plate locations (for fixed versions) from user supplied or computer-generated images and text.
